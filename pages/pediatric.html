    General Pediatric Overview (A006)

    Description

    Pediatric patients are defined as birth until patient's weight based calculations equal the adult dose.

    • Benzodiazepines are an exception to the above statement.

    Medical Treatment Age Ranges

    Age ranges will differ for transport as well as MAAM. Refer to specific guidelines:

    • For specific facility, see DESTINATION GUIDELINE
    • For consent, see CONSENT GUIDELINE
    • For MAAM, see WAIVER GUIDELINE
    • For surgical cricothyrotomy, see AIRWAY GUIDELINE

    Brief Resolved Unexplained Event (BRUE)

    Formerly known as Apparent Life Threatening Event (ALTE)

    1. An infant < 1 year of age with episode frightening to the observer characterized by cyanosis or pale complexion; absent, decreased, or irregular breathing; marked change in muscle tone (hyper- or hypotonia); or altered responsiveness but resolved.
    2. BRUE is diagnosed only when there is no explanation for a qualifying event after conducting appropriate history and physical examination.
    3. Any child with an BRUE SHOULD be transported to ED for evaluation. Monitor vital signs en route.
    4. Make sure to document the following:
      • Observer's impression of color, respirations, tone
      • Was child apneic, cyanotic, or limp?
      • Seizure-like activity?
      • Resuscitation attempted? Spontaneous resolution?
      • Duration of event?

    Special Considerations

    1. For pediatrics, reference Pediatric Field Guide, Broselow Tape, Handtevy Guide, or approved apps.
    2. Airways are smaller, softer, and easier to obstruct or collapse.
    3. Respiratory reserve is small. Minor insults (positioning, emesis, air in stomach) can lead to major problems.
    4. Circulatory reserve is small. Loss of one unit of blood sufficient for severe shock. 500 mL unnecessary fluid can result in acute pulmonary edema.
    5. Vital signs and LOC are difficult to assess. Listen to the parents. They know when changes have occurred.
