    Descriptions
    1. This guideline is designed to assist the prehospital provider effectively assess and manage patient pain and discomfort in the prehospital environment.
    2. The objective of pain management is not the complete relief of all pain, but rather, to make a patient's pain tolerable to allow for assessment, treatment, and transport.
    Precautions
    1. Most pain medications should only be given to hemodynamically stable patients and titrated slowly to effect. In hemodynamically unstable patients, consider giving smaller, incremental doses of FENTANYL or KETAMINE.
    2. Pain medications may cause changes in hemodynamic status and/or respiratory depression (including apnea) that can occur suddenly and without warning and are more common in children and the elderly.
    3. Chest wall rigidity has been reported with rapid administration of FENTANYL
    4. Strongly consider ¬Ω typical dosing in elderly patients or when combining benzodiazepines and opioids
    5. HYDROMORPHONE has a slower onset and much longer period of effect (caution on stacking doses)
    General Patient Care Requirements
    1. All patients receiving pain medication should receive constant ECG, SpO2, EtCO2 and blood pressure monitoring. When this is not possible (i.e., in a back country environment), pulse oximetry and constant verbal engagement with the patient are the minimum monitoring requirements.
    Other Medication Options
    1. The administration of a combination of benzodiazepines and opiates, for the purpose of pain management with associated anxiety is permitted.
      • Ensure that the patient can independently maintain an open airway and normal breathing pattern, maintain normal hemodynamics, respond appropriately to physical stimulation and verbal commands.
    2. Consider an antiemetic if the patient develops nausea or vomiting or has a history of nausea with opiates.
    Special Considerations
    1. In trauma pain is secondary, look for major bleeding or life threats first. Once stabilized consider pain management.
    2. FENTANYL is the first line medication for pain associated with ACS and pregnant patients requiring CARDIOVERSION or TRANSCUTANEOUS PACING, unless allergy exists.
    3. For patients with history of substance abuse, co-morbidities or mild to moderate pain, consider non-narcotic options to manage pain if applicable.
